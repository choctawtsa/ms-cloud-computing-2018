Cloud Computing

noun

the practice of using a network of remote servers hosted on the Internet to store, manage, and process data, rather than a local server or a personal computer.


WHAT DOES IT DO?

You can kind of think of cloud computing as using a computer from another place to store your files or process data, so you don't have to. Average people usually only care about the storage component of cloud computing. There's plenty of reason to, right? People usually have powerful enough hardware on the machine they're running, but some people run out of storage pretty often. People who take lots of pictures, download lots of music, etc. sometimes run out of storage on their device, but luckily, there are multiple cloud computing services out there that will store those files for you. Some services give you some storage for free and require you to pay to get more, and there are some other services that you have to pay to get any storage.





WHAT ARE SOME CLOUD COMPUTING SERVICES?

There are quite a few, actually. These services are mainly divided into two categories: personal or enterprise.

On the personal side, you have services like Apple's iCloud, Google's Drive, or Microsoft's OneDrive. All support storage of files like documents, presentations, pictures, spreadsheets, and much more. Google Drive's cloud computing services also come with things like Google Docs, Slides, Sheets, Keep, Gmail, Translate, Photos, News, Maps, Search, Calendar, and more that can be accessed from virtually anywhere. iCloud comes with Keynote, Numbers, iMovie, GarageBand, Pages, Photo Library, Mail, Calendar, Maps, News, Notes, Reminders, iMessage, Health, Wallet/Apple Pay, Contacts, iTunes, and more. iCloud's services are easier to work with and access on Apple devices (iOS, macOS, etc.), but most services can be accessed via the web or apps. OneDrive works well with Microsoft's Office 365 program and helps store files made in Office applications such as Word, PowerPoint, Excel, Publisher, etc., but Office 365 is a seperate purchase and is not included with OneDrive.
We'd like to note that though iCloud is it's own service, it does use other services to store its users' data. The services iCloud uses used to be Amazon Web Services and Microsoft Azure, but now it seems like they use Google servers and Amazon Web Services.

Then there's the enterprise/business side of things. You'll find services like Google G Suite, Microsoft Azure, Amazon Web Services, IBM Cloud, and more here. These services do some remote computing for their clients as well as storing files the same as personal cloud services. Some of these services offer cool things, such as G Suite offering your own email domain with any purchase.
